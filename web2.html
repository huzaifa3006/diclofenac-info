Diclofenac

Everything you need to know!

Description

WHO Essential Medication List: Not Included

Diclofenac is a phenylacetic acid derivative. It is a product of rational drug design based on the structures of phenylbutazone, mefenamic acid, and indomethacin. The addition of two chlorine groups in the ortho position of the phenyl ring locks the ring in maximal torsion which appears to be related to increased potency. Diclofenac was first approved by the FDA in July 1988 under the trade name Voltaren, marketed by Novartis (previously Ciba-Geigy).

Chemical Structure of Diclofenac

Chemical Formula: C14H11Cl2NO2

Molecular Weight: 296.149 g/mol

Drug Class: Non Steroidal Anti Inflammatory Drug

Indications

  1. Musculoskeletal and Joint Disorders
    • Osteoarthritis (To relieve signs and symptoms such as joint pain, swelling, and stiffness)
    • Rheumatoid arthritis
    • Ankylosing spondylitis
  2. Acute Pain Conditions
    • Postoperative pain (e.g., after dental, or orthopedic, or abdominal surgery)
    • Muscle pain (e.g., sprains, strains)
    • Back pain or low back pain
    • Trauma-related soft tissue inflammation
  3. Gynecological Conditions
    • Primary dysmenorrhea (painful menstrual cramps)
    • Pain related to intrauterine device (IUD) insertion or pelvic inflammatory disease (supportive)
  4. Dental and ENT Conditions
    • Post-dental surgery pain
    • Tonsillitis, pharyngitis-related throat pain (when inflammation is significant)
  5. Gout
    • Acute gout attacks (Short-term management of pain and inflammation)

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Absorption
  • Rapidly absorbed when given as an oral solution, sugar-coated tablets, rectal suppository, or by intramuscular injection.
  • Slow absorption as enteric coated tablet specially when given with food
  • Oral bioavailability: ~50% due to first-pass metabolism.
Distribution
  • 99% bound to plasma proteins (mainly albumin).
  • Volume of distribution (Vd): 0.12--0.17 L/kg.
Metabolism
  • Extensively metabolized in the liver via CYP2C9 to hydroxylated and conjugated metabolites.
  • metabolised to 4′-hydroxydiclofenac, 5-hydroxydiclofenac, 3′-hydroxydiclofenac and 4′,5-dihydroxydiclofenac
Excretion
  • Excrete as glucuronide and sulfate conjugates:
    • Urine (about 60%)
    • Bile (about 35%);
    • less than 1% is excreted as unchanged diclofenac.
  • Terminal Half life : 1-2 hrs

In Vitro--In Vivo Correlation (IVIVC):

Diclofenac, a BCS Class II drug, exhibits low solubility and high permeability, making its dissolution rate the critical determinant of bioavailability. Immediate-release formulations tend to show rapid dissolution and correlate well with early Tmax values. In contrast, extended-release formulations show slower in vitro dissolution, which aligns with flattened and delayed plasma concentration--time curves.

IVIVC models are especially useful in predicting the performance of new sustained-release diclofenac formulations. A Level A correlation has been demonstrated in some studies, indicating a point-to-point relationship between in vitro dissolution and in vivo absorption. Such models can be used to justify biowaivers and reduce clinical testing during product development.

Mechanism of Action

• Pain Modulation:
PGE2 is the main prostaglandin involved in nociception. It sensitizes peripheral nerves via EP1 (Gq) and EP4 (Gs) receptors, enhancing TRPV1, sodium channels, and reducing potassium currents---lowering the threshold for pain. Centrally, EP2 receptors increase excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, CGRP, substance P) and enhance NMDA/AMPA activity while inhibiting glycinergic neurons, contributing to central sensitization.

• Inflammation:
PGE2 and PGI2 promote vasodilation and immune cell recruitment via EP2, EP4, and IP receptors. They also influence T-helper cell activation, playing a role in inflammatory diseases like arthritis. PGD2 aids cytokine release through DP1 receptors.

• Fever:
PGE2 crosses the blood-brain barrier and activates EP3 receptors in the hypothalamus, increasing body temperature. NSAIDs reduce fever by blocking PGE2 synthesis.

Adverse Drug Reactions

Mild (Common, often self-limiting):

  • Gastrointestinal: Epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea (7.6%)
  • CNS-related: Headache, dizziness (0.7%)
  • Skin: Rash, pruritus
  • Local (Suppositories/injections): Itching, burning, injection site irritation or necrosis
  • Ocular (oral use): Dry, gritty eyes

Moderate (Require intervention or discontinuation)

  • Gastrointestinal: Peptic ulcers, GI bleeding, colonic ulceration, pseudomembranous colitis
  • Hepatic: Elevated liver enzymes, clinical hepatitis (often within 6 months; more common in women)
  • Renal: Nephrotic syndrome, renal papillary necrosis
  • Haematological: Thrombocytopenia, neutropenia, haemolytic anaemia
  • Skin: Bullous dermatitis, erythema multiforme
  • Electrolyte disturbances: Hyponatraemia, SIADH-like syndrome

Severe (Potentially life-threatening or requiring hospitalization)

  • Liver: Fulminant hepatitis, hepatic failure (rare but reported)
  • Blood dyscrasias: Aplastic anaemia (10× increased risk), agranulocytosis
  • Ocular (topical use): Corneal melting, ulceration, perforation
  • Bleeding disorders: Spontaneous bleeding, prolonged bleeding time

Contraindications

Avoid Diclofenac in:

  • Active peptic ulcer or GI bleeding
  • NSAID hypersensitivity (e.g., aspirin-induced asthma)
  • Severe heart failure or ischemic heart disease
  • 3rd trimester of pregnancy
  • Severe hepatic or renal impairment
  • Porphyric patients

Pregnancy & Lactation

Pregnancy:

  • Old FDA Category: C (1st & 2nd trimester), D (3rd trimester)
  • New FDA Labeling (PLLR):
    • Risk in 3rd trimester: Can cause premature closure of ductus arteriosus (Avoid completely.)
    • In 1st/2nd trimesters: Use only if absolutely necessary.

Lactation:

  • Safe during breastfeeding? ✅ Yes (with caution)
    • Diclofenac is excreted in very low amounts in breast milk.
  • Considered safe for short-term use at the lowest effective dose.

Patient Counseling Points for Diclofenac

  1. Take With Food or Milk
    To reduce the risk of gastric irritation, take diclofenac with meals or milk, especially in oral forms. Do not lie down immediately after taking it.
  2. Avoid Alcohol:
    Alcohol increases the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding when taken with diclofenac. Avoid alcohol use, especially during long-term therapy.
  3. Stick to the Prescribed Dose and Duration
    Do not exceed the recommended dose or continue taking diclofenac longer than prescribed, as prolonged use can increase the risk of kidney, liver, heart, and GI complications.
  4. Stay Hydrated
    Maintain adequate fluid intake during treatment. Diclofenac can affect kidney function, especially in the elderly or dehydrated patients.
  5. Do Not Use With Other NSAIDs
    Avoid using other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (like ibuprofen or naproxen) unless advised by your doctor, as this increases the risk of GI bleeding and renal toxicity.
  6. Report Any Alarming Symptoms
    Immediately report:
    • Black or tarry stools
    • Vomiting blood
    • Severe stomach pain
    • Shortness of breath, chest pain
    • Swelling of the hands, legs, or face
  7. Special Precaution During Pregnancy
    Diclofenac is not safe in the third trimester due to the risk of premature closure of the fetal ductus arteriosus. Discuss with your doctor if you are pregnant, planning pregnancy, or breastfeeding.
  8. Topical Use Guidance
    • For gels or patches
    • Apply only on intact skin.
    • Avoid exposure to sun or heat on the treated area.
    • Wash hands before and after application.
    • Do not use under occlusive dressings unless instructed.
  9. Avoid Missing Doses
    If a dose is missed, take it as soon as you remember. Do not double the dose to make up for a missed one.
  10. Caution While Driving
    Diclofenac can cause dizziness or drowsiness in some people. Avoid driving or operating machinery until you know how the drug affects you.

FAQs - Diclofenac

1. Can I use Diclofenac with paracetamol?

✅ Yes. They can be used together for better pain control as they work via different mechanisms.

2. How long can I use Diclofenac gel?

⏳ Usually 7-14 days. Prolonged use should be done only under medical supervision.

3. What if I miss a dose?

⏱️ Take it as soon as you remember. If it's near the next dose, skip the missed one - don't double the dose.

4. Can I drive after taking Diclofenac?

🚗 Usually yes but avoid driving if you feel dizzy or drowsy.

5. Is Diclofenac safe for long-term use?

⚠️ Not recommended without medical advice due to risks like GI bleeding, kidney, and heart issues.
